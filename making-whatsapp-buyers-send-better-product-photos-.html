How we redesigned the buyer journey to get clearer requirements and fewer empty inquiries

Introduction

Every day, thousands of buyers reach out to suppliers on IndiaMART through WhatsApp. It's fast, familiar, and works on every phone in India. But there was a problem we couldn't ignore.

When buyers initiated conversations, many were sending blank messages or vague text inquiries without any product details. Suppliers were left guessing what the buyer actually wanted. Meanwhile, buyers who did have photos of the products they needed often didn't think to share them upfront. This meant longer back-and-forth conversations, delayed responses, and frustrated users on both sides.

We call these initial buyer messages "BLs" (Buyer Leads). The quality of that first message matters enormously. A photo-rich BL helps suppliers respond faster and more accurately. A blank or text-only BL? That's a missed opportunity.

So we set out to redesign the WhatsApp buyer journey with one clear goal: encourage buyers to share product photos early, and reduce the number of blank inquiries. Here's how we did it, and what we learned along the way.

The Problem with Blank and Text-Only Leads üöß

When a buyer contacts a supplier through WhatsApp, that first message sets the tone for the entire conversation. Our data showed two major issues:

1. Too many blank leads: Buyers would tap "Contact Supplier" and send a message with no context. The supplier would receive a notification, open WhatsApp, and find nothing actionable. They'd have to reply with "What product do you need?" and wait. Often, the buyer had moved on.

2. Missing visual context: Even when buyers sent text, they rarely included photos. In B2B commerce, especially in categories like machinery, furniture, or custom parts, a picture is worth a thousand words. A buyer looking for a "steel bracket" could mean dozens of different products. A photo makes it instantly clear.

The result? Lower response rates, longer lead times, and wasted effort for both buyers and suppliers.

We needed to guide buyers toward better behavior without adding friction. The challenge was doing this within WhatsApp's constraints and India's diverse connectivity landscape.

Redesigning the Buyer Journey üõ†Ô∏è

We redesigned the flow to make photo sharing feel natural and valuable. Here's what we changed:

Step 1: Prompting for Photos Early

When a buyer initiates contact, we now show a clear, visual prompt encouraging them to share a photo of the product they need. The message is simple: "Share a photo for faster quotes."

The new prompt nudges buyers to share product photos right at the start

This small change had a big impact. By framing photo sharing as a way to get faster, better responses, we aligned with what buyers already wanted.

Step 2: Inline Photo Upload

We made uploading photos as easy as possible. Instead of asking buyers to navigate away or switch apps, we integrated a photo picker directly into the flow. One tap, choose from gallery or camera, done.

Buyers can now upload photos in just one tap

This reduced cognitive load and kept the momentum going. Buyers didn't have to think - they just followed the natural next step.

Step 3: Confirmation and Context

After uploading a photo, buyers see a confirmation screen showing the image they selected. This gives them a chance to review before sending, reducing accidental or unclear uploads.

Buyers can review their photo before sending to suppliers

We also added an optional text field for additional context. Buyers could specify quantity, delivery location, or any custom requirements. But the photo came first.

Step 4: Rich Lead Delivered to Supplier

On the supplier side, the experience improved dramatically. Instead of a blank or vague message, suppliers now receive a rich, photo-first lead with clear visual context.

Suppliers get clear visual context from the first message

This enabled them to respond with accurate quotes faster, improving conversion rates and buyer satisfaction.

The Results üìä

After rolling out the redesigned journey, we tracked two key metrics:

Photo BLs increased significantly. More buyers were sharing product photos in their first message. This meant suppliers had the context they needed to respond quickly and accurately.

Blank BLs dropped sharply. By guiding the conversation with clear prompts and easy actions, we reduced the number of empty inquiries. Fewer dead-end conversations, more productive exchanges.

What We Learned Along the Way üí°

Building this feature taught us several important lessons about user behavior and product design:

1. Nudges work better than gates: We didn't force buyers to upload photos. We encouraged it. This kept the flow flexible while still driving the behavior we wanted.

2. Context matters more than features: Buyers didn't need more buttons or options. They needed to understand why sharing a photo would help them. Once we framed it as "get faster quotes," adoption improved.

3. Small friction adds up: Even minor obstacles, like navigating to a separate screen or switching apps, can kill momentum. Keeping everything inline and simple made a huge difference.

4. Visual feedback builds confidence: Showing buyers their uploaded photo before sending reassured them and reduced errors. It's a small detail, but it mattered.

Visual feedback throughout the journey builds user confidence

5. Designing for India means designing for WhatsApp: In the Indian B2B space, WhatsApp isn't just a channel - it's the channel. Optimizing for this platform meant respecting its constraints (like message size and loading times) while maximizing its strengths (like familiarity and reach).

Conclusion

Redesigning the WhatsApp buyer journey wasn't about adding flashy features or complex technology. It was about understanding user behavior and removing friction at the right moments.

By prompting buyers to share photos early, making uploads effortless, and providing clear visual feedback, we increased photo-rich leads and reduced blank inquiries. Suppliers got better context. Buyers got faster responses. The entire conversation improved.

This work reminds us that great product design often comes down to the basics: understand the problem, simplify the solution, and test relentlessly. In a market as dynamic and mobile-first as India, those principles matter more than ever.

If you're building for B2B commerce or working on conversational flows, think carefully about that first user action. It sets the tone for everything that follows. Make it easy. Make it valuable. Make it visual.

And always, always test with real users.
