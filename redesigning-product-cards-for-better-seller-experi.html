Redesigning Product Cards for Better Seller Experience

How a UI refresh helped sellers see more product details without clutter, and why small design changes matter at scale.

Introduction

Imagine managing hundreds of products on a marketplace platform. You log in to check your inventory, and each product card shows just five key details - some truncated, some color-coded in ways you've gotten used to over the years, but not quite intuitive. You want to quickly spot what's missing, what needs updating, and where to focus your attention. But the interface feels dated, and finding that information takes more effort than it should.

This was the reality for thousands of sellers on IndiaMART. The product card design on the Manage Product section had served its purpose well, but after years of incremental updates, it had become a patchwork of features. Colors meant different things on seller versus admin views. Important details were getting cut off. The layout didn't follow modern design principles.

The challenge wasn't that the old design was broken - it was that it could be so much better. So we decided to rebuild it from scratch, focusing on space optimization, visual clarity, and helping sellers take the right actions faster. Here's how we approached the redesign and what we learned from testing it with real users.

The Problem with the Old Design üé®

The existing product card had evolved organically over several years. Each new feature or requirement was added on top of the previous structure, creating a design that worked but wasn't optimal.

Here's what sellers and our production team were dealing with:

  • Limited visibility: Only 5 product specifications (ISQs) were shown at once, with unfilled config items and other filled attributes competing for space
  • Inconsistent color coding: Green for config items, grey for others on seller view; but on admin view, different colors (green, brown, black, grey) represented different attribute types - confusing and hard to remember
  • No clear indication of mandatory fields: Sellers couldn't immediately tell which specifications they had to fill versus nice-to-have details
  • Cluttered layout: Share buttons, audit icons, and product scores were scattered across the card without a clear visual hierarchy
  • Price display issues: Prices appeared without proper formatting (no comma separators), making large numbers hard to read at a glance
  • Space inefficiency: The linear product score scale and scattered UI elements consumed valuable screen real estate

Perhaps most importantly, there was no visual distinction for mandatory specifications - a compliance issue that put both sellers and the platform at risk.

The old product card UI - functional but showing signs of accumulated design debt

The Solution: Modern Design Meets Seller Needs üí°

Rather than another incremental update, we decided on a comprehensive UI/UX refresh. But we didn't want to reinvent the wheel - the information hierarchy and media placement were working well. Instead, we focused on typography, iconography, color usage, and space optimization.

Design Principles We Followed

We built the new design around IndiaMART's Style Guide v1.0, ensuring consistency across the platform. Every color choice, font size, and spacing decision now followed documented standards rather than ad-hoc decisions.

Key Changes Implemented

More information, better organized:

  • Increased ISQ display from 5 to 6 specifications (filled + unfilled)
  • Unfilled mandatory, config, and key specifications now appear in amber-colored capsules - impossible to miss
  • Regular unfilled specs show in subtle grey capsules
  • Asterisk (*) indicator next to mandatory fields with a tooltip - solving the compliance requirement

Smarter use of space:

  • Replaced the linear product score scale with a circular scale - same information, less space
  • Grouped share icons together with Preview and QC buttons in the top-right corner (ordered by actual usage data: Copy, WhatsApp, Facebook, LinkedIn, Twitter)
  • Replaced "Use AI to Audit" with a simpler "QC" button for quality control

Better readability:

  • Increased font size for key product details that sellers need to scan quickly
  • Reduced heading sizes to create better visual balance
  • Added digit grouping with commas for prices (e.g., 1,50,000 instead of 150000)
  • Removed price capsule background when price is available - cleaner look

Consistency improvements:

  • Removed color coding from ISQs on admin view (kept helpful tooltips for production team)
  • Standardized ISQ display order across both seller and admin views: Mandatory Unfilled ‚Üí Config Unfilled ‚Üí Key Unfilled ‚Üí All Filled ‚Üí Regular Unfilled
  • Deprecated the "half-card" UI that appeared for certain product categories - all products now use the full card layout

Testing the Design with Real Sellers üß™

We didn't roll this out to everyone immediately. Instead, we ran an A/B test to measure the real impact.

The Test Setup

  • Test group (Even GLIDs): Saw the new UI
  • Control group (Odd GLIDs): Continued with the old UI
  • Who was affected: Both sellers and admin/employee logins
  • What we monitored: Key KPIs including fill rates for price and specifications, user engagement, and any negative signals

This approach let us validate our design assumptions with real usage data before committing to a full rollout.

What We Expected

The hypothesis was straightforward: better visual design and clearer information hierarchy should help sellers take action more easily. Specifically:

  • Higher fill rates for mandatory specifications (now clearly marked with asterisk)
  • Improved fill rates for price (better formatted and more visible)
  • Better engagement with specifications (showing 6 instead of 5, with unfilled items in capsules)
  • No negative impact on existing workflows

Why This Matters Beyond Aesthetics üéØ

Design refreshes can feel superficial, but this one tackled real business needs:

For sellers:

  • Faster identification of incomplete product information
  • Clear understanding of what's mandatory versus optional
  • Less cognitive load when managing dozens or hundreds of products
  • Professional, modern interface that reflects well on the platform

For the platform:

  • Legal compliance through clear mandatory field indicators
  • Better data quality as sellers fill more specifications
  • Reduced support queries about what needs to be filled
  • Consistent design language across the seller experience

For the production team:

  • Retained helpful tooltips while removing visual clutter
  • Streamlined QC process with the new "QC" button
  • Consistent view across admin and seller interfaces

Conclusion

The product card redesign shows how thoughtful UI/UX work can solve multiple problems at once. What started as "let's modernize the design" became an opportunity to improve information density, solve compliance requirements, and create a more consistent seller experience.

The A/B testing approach gave us confidence that we weren't just making things look better - we were making them work better. By monitoring key metrics closely, we could validate that the new design helped sellers complete their profiles more effectively.

Design isn't just about aesthetics; it's about reducing friction. Every truncated label, every unclear status indicator, every misaligned button is a small moment of confusion that adds up across thousands of sellers and millions of sessions. This redesign removed many of those small frictions.

As we continue monitoring the test results and prepare for a potential full rollout, the bigger lesson is clear: legacy interfaces need periodic refreshes, not just to keep up with design trends, but to serve users better as their needs evolve and platforms mature.
